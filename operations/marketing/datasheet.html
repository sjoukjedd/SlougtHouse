Next-Generation Ambulatory Monitoring System

VU-AMS — Ambulatory Stress Monitor
ECG + ICG + EDA + PPG + Motion

The VU-AMS is the world's only ambulatory wearable that simultaneously records Impedance Cardiography (ICG) and ECG in a chest-worn form factor, delivering cardiac output, stroke volume, and baroreflex sensitivity in daily-life conditions. The next generation adds skin conductance, PPG/SpO₂, body temperature, and 9-DOF inertial sensing in the same 55 × 55 mm device.

ICG — The Unique Differentiator

Impedance Cardiography is the only non-invasive technique that provides direct haemodynamic parameters in ambulatory conditions. No other wearable device delivers these measurements outside a clinical or laboratory setting.

Safety standard
The VU-AMS is battery-operated exclusively during patient contact. The USB-C charging port is fitted with a mechanical protective cover that must be removed before connecting to mains power, ensuring no simultaneous patient connection and mains coupling. ICG patient circuitry is galvanically isolated from the signal conditioning chain via a 1:1 bifilar-wound transformer (MnZn ferrite, Faraday-shielded) achieving >120 dB CMRR at 32 kHz. Protective earth is not applicable (battery-only, no mains connection during use).

Research Applications
Occupational stress research
Ambulatory cardiovascular and autonomic responses to workplace demands. CO, SV, PEP, HRV and SCL during naturalistic work conditions.
Psychophysiology & emotion
Full sympathetic–parasympathetic profiling. EDA/SCL for arousal, BRS for vagal tone, PEP for β-adrenergic activation.
Cardiac rehabilitation
Post-event haemodynamic monitoring in daily life. CO and SV trajectories over weeks without clinic visits.
Sports & exercise physiology
Real-field cardiac output measurement during training. 9-DOF IMU for activity classification and movement artefact correction.

Validation & Scientific Basis
ICG methodology
VU-AMS ICG algorithms are validated against gold-standard thermodilution and Fick methods. Published in peer-reviewed journals since 1995. Signal processing follows Kubicek impedance model with Bernstein–Lemmens corrections.
HRV standards
HRV analysis complies with the 1996 Task Force guidelines (ESC/NASPE). Time-domain (SDNN, RMSSD), frequency-domain (LF, HF, LF/HF ratio) and non-linear indices (SD1/SD2, ApEn).
EDA measurement
Skin conductance electrodes follow LEDALAB and SCSR standards. Tonic/phasic decomposition uses established CDA and DDA methods. Sampling complies with the 32 Hz minimum for SCR detection.
